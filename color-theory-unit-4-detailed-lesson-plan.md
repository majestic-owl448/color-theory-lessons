# Unit 4 Detailed Lesson Plan: Human Vision and Color Blindness

## Unit summary
This unit teaches the learner that color is not experienced in exactly the same way by every viewer. The goal is to move beginner designers beyond the idea that "if it looks fine to me, it is fine for everyone." Learners should understand the basic parts of the eye that are relevant to color perception, the major categories of color vision deficiency, and the practical interface problems that appear when design relies too heavily on color alone.

This unit should stay practical. It is not a biology course. It should give the learner just enough vision science to make better interface decisions, especially in UI states, charts, forms, maps, and dashboards.

## Unit goal
Teach the learner to design with awareness of human color perception differences and the most common color vision deficiencies.

## Unit outcomes
By the end of Unit 4, the learner should be able to:
- explain in plain language how the eye and brain contribute to color perception
- describe the practical role of cones in seeing color
- identify the major types of color vision deficiency at a beginner-friendly level
- explain why red and green is only part of the story
- use simulation tools to inspect interface problems under different vision conditions
- identify designs that rely too much on color alone
- improve interfaces by adding labels, shapes, icons, patterns, or stronger contrast
- make more robust choices for status colors, charts, and form feedback
- explain why inclusive color design is a usability issue, not just a compliance task

## Unit structure
- **Target lesson count:** 6 lessons
- **Estimated lesson length:** 12 to 18 minutes each
- **Estimated unit duration:** 2 to 2.5 hours including milestone
- **Unit milestone:** Design Beyond Your Own Eyes
- **Milestone badge/title:** Inclusive Vision Designer

## Pedagogical approach
This unit should teach through empathy, simulation, and repair:
1. introduce the core idea that color perception varies across people
2. explain only the most useful human-vision basics
3. let the learner compare interfaces through simulated vision modes
4. show common failures that appear when meaning depends on color alone
5. require the learner to repair those failures using multiple cues

The unit should repeatedly reinforce one central idea: inclusive color design is not about memorizing medical terms. It is about making interfaces understandable for more people.

# Lesson 1: How Humans Perceive Color

## Lesson purpose
Introduce the most practical parts of human color perception so the learner understands that color is a human interpretation, not just a property of pixels.

## Duration
12 to 15 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- explain that seeing color involves light, the eye, and the brain
- name the basic eye structures relevant to color perception at a simple level
- describe the role of cones in color vision
- explain why two people may not perceive the same interface in the same way

## Key concepts
- color perception is constructed by the visual system
- light enters the eye and is processed by specialized cells
- cones help us distinguish color information
- vision differences affect practical design outcomes
- design should account for variation in perception

## Key terms
- retina
- cone
- rod
- optic nerve
- color perception
- visual system
- color vision

## Lesson explanation outline
- A digital screen emits light, but the human eye and brain interpret that light.
- The retina contains rods and cones.
- Cones are especially relevant to color perception.
- Rods are more associated with low-light vision and are less central for color detail.
- The brain interprets visual signals, which means color is not just a raw fact of the screen.
- Because perception depends on human biology, different people can experience the same interface differently.
- Good design takes this seriously instead of assuming a single "normal" viewer.

## Primary interaction
**See the path of color information**
- Show a simplified diagram of light entering the eye.
- Let the learner click through the steps:
  1. light from screen
  2. eye receives light
  3. retina processes signal
  4. brain interprets signal
- Each step reveals one short explanation and one practical design implication.

## Guided activity
**Which part matters here?**
Present short statements and ask the learner to connect them to the right concept:
- helps detect color differences
- helps in low-light conditions
- receives the image in the back of the eye
- sends visual information onward for interpretation

## Practice task
**Explain it simply**
Ask the learner to complete the sentence:
"A screen can show the same pixels to everyone, but people may still perceive them differently because..."
The response can be chosen from several guided options or filled in with a short sentence.

## Quiz/check for understanding
- Which cells are most relevant to color perception?
- Is color only a property of the screen, or also of human perception?
- Why might two users experience the same interface differently?

## Success criteria
The learner can explain in simple terms that color perception depends on the human visual system and is not identical for every viewer.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- thinking color is only a software or display problem
- treating the brain's interpretation as irrelevant to design
- overcomplicating the biology and losing the practical lesson

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Right. The screen provides light, but the visual system interprets it."
- "Good. Designers do not need full anatomy here, only enough to understand why perception varies."
- "Try focusing on the practical result: different users may not experience your colors the same way."

# Lesson 2: What Color Vision Deficiency Is

## Lesson purpose
Introduce color vision deficiency as a family of perceptual differences rather than a single red-green problem.

## Duration
12 to 15 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- explain what color vision deficiency means at a beginner-friendly level
- recognize that there are multiple types, not one single condition
- describe the difference between complete absence and reduced sensitivity in simple terms
- reject oversimplified assumptions about "everyone sees red and green the same way"

## Key concepts
- color vision deficiency affects how certain color differences are perceived
- there are multiple categories and severities
- some conditions involve reduced sensitivity, while others involve missing response patterns
- the practical design question is not diagnosis, but interface robustness

## Key terms
- color vision deficiency
- protanopia
- protanomaly
- deuteranopia
- deuteranomaly
- tritanopia
- tritanomaly
- achromatopsia
- deficiency
- simulation

## Lesson explanation outline
- Color vision deficiency is a broad term for differences in how some colors are distinguished.
- The most commonly discussed cases involve red-green distinctions, but that is not the whole story.
- Some people have reduced sensitivity rather than a complete absence of a response pattern.
- Blue-yellow related deficiencies also exist.
- Total or near-total lack of typical color perception is much rarer, but important to mention.
- Designers do not need to memorize medical nuance for its own sake.
- They do need to understand that "looks distinct to me" is not enough evidence.

## Primary interaction
**Family tree of vision differences**
- Show an interactive set of cards for the major categories.
- The learner clicks each card to reveal:
  - simple description
  - common design risk
  - example UI pair that may become harder to distinguish
- Keep the descriptions short and practical.

## Guided activity
**Not just red and green**
Ask the learner to sort examples into three buckets:
- common red-green issue
- blue-yellow issue
- extreme or broad color perception issue

## Practice task
**Challenge the assumption**
Show five statements such as:
- "Color blindness just means someone cannot see any color."
- "It only affects red and green."
- "If my red and green are bright enough, they are safe."
The learner marks each statement as accurate, misleading, or false and gets an explanation.

## Quiz/check for understanding
- Is color vision deficiency one single condition?
- Which statement is more accurate: "All color blindness is red-green" or "There are multiple kinds of color vision deficiency"?
- Why is it risky to rely on your own perception as the only test?

## Success criteria
The learner can explain that color vision deficiency includes multiple kinds of perception differences and that design should account for more than one pattern.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- using "color blindness" as if it refers to one identical experience
- assuming all users either see colors normally or not at all
- memorizing names without understanding the practical implication

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Correct. The most common discussions focus on red-green differences, but that is not the whole picture."
- "Good. The design lesson is about robustness, not medical memorization."
- "Try asking: would this still work if these colors felt less distinct?"

# Lesson 3: The Major Types and What They Mean for Interfaces

## Lesson purpose
Teach the learner the most important high-level categories of color vision deficiency and connect each one to practical design risks.

## Duration
15 to 18 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- distinguish the major categories at a practical level
- associate each category with common interface risks
- identify status colors, chart colors, and comparison tasks that may break under different conditions
- explain why designers should test, not guess

## Key concepts
- protan-related conditions affect certain red distinctions
- deutan-related conditions affect certain green distinctions
- tritan-related conditions affect certain blue-yellow distinctions
- achromatopsia can make hue-based systems collapse dramatically
- practical failure often appears when meaning depends on hue difference without backup cues

## Key terms
- protan
- deutan
- tritan
- achromatopsia
- status color
- chart key
- legend
- redundancy
- backup cue

## Lesson explanation outline
- The unit should explain categories using plain language and real examples.
- Protan and deutan conditions are often discussed together because they can both affect red-green distinction tasks.
- Tritan-related issues can affect blue-yellow distinctions and are often ignored by beginners.
- Achromatopsia is rarer but highlights an important principle: if hue carries all the meaning, the system can fail badly.
- Designers should think in terms of task breakdown:
  - Can the user tell success from error?
  - Can the user tell one chart series from another?
  - Can the user tell selected from unselected?
  - Can the user tell a link from body text?

## Primary interaction
**Simulated interface comparison gallery**
- Show the same interface in multiple simulated modes:
  - typical view
  - protan-like simulation
  - deutan-like simulation
  - tritan-like simulation
  - grayscale or extreme reduction mode
- The learner toggles between them and logs which distinctions weaken or disappear.

## Guided activity
**Which task breaks first?**
For several interfaces, the learner chooses what practical task becomes harder:
- identifying error state
- comparing chart series
- reading a heatmap
- spotting selected navigation item
- distinguishing active and disabled controls

## Practice task
**Map the risk**
Provide three mini interfaces:
1. form validation with red and green text only
2. line chart with color-only legend
3. tab bar with subtle blue-gray selected state
The learner writes or selects what could fail under different vision conditions.

## Quiz/check for understanding
- Which category is commonly associated with red-green distinction problems?
- Are blue-yellow related issues relevant to interface design?
- What is the core design lesson across all categories?

## Success criteria
The learner can connect major categories to likely interface risks and describe failure in terms of user tasks.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- trying to memorize medical detail instead of interface implications
- ignoring tritan-related issues completely
- thinking rare cases are irrelevant to principle-driven design
- describing failure only as a color shift instead of a task failure

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Good. What matters most is the user task that stops working."
- "Right. It is not enough to say the colors look different. Ask whether the interface still communicates clearly."
- "Try naming the exact comparison the user must make."

# Lesson 4: Why Color Alone Fails

## Lesson purpose
Teach the learner to recognize one of the most common inclusive design failures: encoding meaning in color without any supporting cue.

## Duration
12 to 15 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- identify interfaces that rely on color alone for meaning
- explain why this is fragile even beyond color vision deficiency
- add alternative cues such as labels, icons, shapes, patterns, position, or text
- evaluate whether a design still works when hue information is weakened

## Key concepts
- color-only communication is fragile
- backup cues improve comprehension for many users
- inclusive design also helps users in glare, low quality screens, tiredness, and quick scanning situations
- a robust interface gives more than one clue

## Key terms
- use of color
- backup cue
- redundancy
- label
- icon
- pattern
- selected state
- semantic state

## Lesson explanation outline
- A common design mistake is to use hue as the only carrier of meaning.
- Examples include:
  - error and success shown only with red and green
  - selected state shown only with a color change
  - charts distinguished only by color
  - required fields marked only by color
- These fail not only for color vision differences, but also in other real situations such as poor lighting or quick scanning.
- Robust interfaces add at least one supporting cue:
  - text label
  - icon
  - shape difference
  - underline or border
  - pattern or texture
  - layout change

## Primary interaction
**Color-only detector**
- Present 8 small UI examples.
- The learner clicks every example where meaning depends only on color.
- After each selection, the app explains what alternative cue would improve it.

## Guided activity
**Add one more cue**
For each example, the learner chooses the best supporting cue:
- icon plus label
- underline for link state
- pattern for chart series
- border or checkmark for selected item
- text feedback for form validation

## Practice task
**Repair the fragile interface**
Give the learner a small interface with three color-only problems. Ask them to pick from a toolbox of cues and apply them.

## Quiz/check for understanding
- What does "color alone" mean in interface design?
- Name two ways to reinforce meaning without depending only on hue.
- Why is a backup cue useful even for users without color vision deficiency?

## Success criteria
The learner can spot color-only communication and propose stronger alternatives for most examples.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- believing color-only is acceptable if the contrast is high
- adding decorative icons that do not actually clarify meaning
- thinking text labels always make visual structure worse

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Correct. The problem is not the color itself. The problem is that nothing else carries the meaning."
- "Good. A label or icon works because it adds a second channel of information."
- "Try asking: if I removed the hue difference, what would still tell the user what is happening?"

# Lesson 5: Designing Better States, Charts, and Feedback

## Lesson purpose
Help the learner apply inclusive color thinking to common interface patterns that often fail under simulation.

## Duration
15 to 18 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- improve status colors and semantic systems with supporting cues
- strengthen charts and graphs so series remain distinguishable
- make form states easier to interpret under varied vision conditions
- choose stronger combinations of hue, contrast, and non-color cues

## Key concepts
- semantic states need more than a color swatch
- charts should use labels, direct annotation, and pattern where useful
- forms need understandable feedback, not just colored borders
- inclusive design is often about combining several small improvements

## Key terms
- semantic color
- success state
- warning state
- error state
- info state
- direct label
- chart series
- annotation
- pattern fill
- validation feedback

## Lesson explanation outline
- Beginners often learn color by thinking of fixed semantic hues: red means error, green means success, yellow means warning.
- That is only part of the job.
- The full design task is to create a state system that stays understandable under multiple conditions.
- In charts, relying only on color can create confusion when multiple series become similar.
- In forms, a red border alone often leaves too much ambiguity.
- A stronger system uses combinations such as:
  - icon plus label plus accessible contrast
  - direct chart labels or line styles
  - helper text plus clear heading plus border state

## Primary interaction
**State system workshop**
- The learner edits a success, warning, error, and info system.
- For each state they can adjust:
  - hue
  - text label
  - icon
  - border style
  - contrast strength
- The app then shows simulated modes and task-based feedback.

## Guided activity
**Fix the chart**
Present a line chart, bar chart, and pie or segmented chart with weak reliance on color. Ask the learner to improve each one by choosing tools such as:
- direct labels
- pattern fills
- line styles
- stronger separation
- legend improvements
- reduced series count

## Practice task
**Form feedback redesign**
Show a sign-up form where only border colors indicate problems and success. The learner must revise the form so users can quickly understand:
- which field needs attention
- what is wrong
- what action to take next

## Quiz/check for understanding
- Why is a green border alone a weak success message?
- Name two ways to make chart series easier to distinguish.
- Which is stronger: red border only, or red border plus icon plus clear error text?

## Success criteria
The learner can redesign common UI patterns so meaning survives beyond hue alone.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- adding too many cues and creating clutter
- assuming every chart needs pattern everywhere
- adding labels without improving the task flow
- treating every problem as a contrast issue when structure is the real issue

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Good. The user now has more than one clue, which makes the system more reliable."
- "Right. Direct labeling often helps more than adding more colors."
- "Try choosing the smallest set of cues that makes the task clear."

# Lesson 6: Simulation, Testing, and Everyday Workflow

## Lesson purpose
Teach the learner how to incorporate simulation and inclusive color checks into normal design practice.

## Duration
12 to 15 minutes

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson, the learner should be able to:
- explain the purpose and limits of color vision simulations
- use simulation as a design review step rather than a final afterthought
- describe a practical workflow for checking screens, states, and charts
- explain why simulation should be paired with labels, contrast checks, and task-based thinking

## Key concepts
- simulation is a useful approximation, not a perfect substitute for real user testing
- inclusive review should happen early and repeatedly
- task-based checking is more useful than staring at color swatches alone
- simulation, contrast review, and backup cues work best together

## Key terms
- simulation
- workflow
- review pass
- task check
- approximation
- inclusive design
- user test
- robustness

## Lesson explanation outline
- A simulation tool can help a designer see likely problem areas.
- It does not perfectly reproduce a real person's experience.
- That means simulation is valuable, but should be used carefully.
- The practical workflow should be simple enough to repeat often:
  1. review screen in normal mode
  2. switch to simulated modes
  3. look for task failures, not just color changes
  4. check whether color alone carries meaning
  5. add labels, icons, patterns, or stronger contrast where needed
  6. test the revised version again
- Designers should integrate this into regular review, especially for states, charts, forms, dashboards, and navigation.

## Primary interaction
**Inclusive review checklist simulator**
- Show a screen and a review checklist next to it.
- The learner moves through the checklist and flags issues.
- The app scores completeness of the review process, not just the final answer.

## Guided activity
**Which review step is missing?**
Present mini case studies where a designer missed an important step. Ask the learner to identify the missing action:
- no simulation check
- no label backup
- no task-based testing
- no chart distinction improvement
- no form message clarity

## Practice task
**Build your own review routine**
The learner arranges a small workflow from mixed cards into the best order for day-to-day design practice.

## Quiz/check for understanding
- Is simulation a perfect representation of every user's experience?
- What should the learner look for during simulated review?
- Why should inclusive color checks happen before final polish?

## Success criteria
The learner can describe and use a repeatable workflow for checking color robustness in real interface work.

## Common mistakes to anticipate
- treating simulation as a one-time compliance step
- focusing on whether colors look strange instead of whether tasks remain clear
- assuming simulation replaces user empathy or user testing

## Suggested feedback lines
- "Correct. Simulation is useful, but it is still an approximation."
- "Good. The key question is whether the user can still complete the task."
- "Try making inclusive review part of the normal design loop, not a final emergency patch."

# Unit milestone: Design Beyond Your Own Eyes

## Milestone purpose
Require the learner to apply the whole unit to a realistic screen and redesign it so it remains understandable across multiple simulated vision conditions.

## Estimated duration
20 to 30 minutes

## Milestone scenario
The learner receives a flawed product screen such as a settings page, analytics dashboard, or sign-up flow containing problems like:
- red and green status chips with no labels
- low-distinction chart series
- selected tabs shown only by hue change
- form validation shown only through colored outlines
- small legend items that depend entirely on color identity

The screen can be toggled through several simulated modes.

## Milestone tasks
The learner must:
1. inspect the screen in typical mode
2. inspect the screen in multiple simulated modes
3. identify at least five task-level failures
4. repair the design using better cues and clearer structure
5. explain the reasoning behind each repair

## Deliverable format
The milestone can be structured as a guided redesign with:
- clickable issue markers
- a toolbox of repair options
- short written explanations
- before-and-after comparison

## Milestone success criteria
To pass the milestone, the learner should:
- correctly identify the main color-reliance failures
- improve at least three types of interface pattern, such as chart, form, and state system
- use more than one type of supporting cue
- demonstrate that the revised design still works better under simulation
- explain changes in terms of usability, not just aesthetics

## Milestone feedback examples
- "You identified the problem correctly, but the repair still depends too much on hue. Try adding a second cue."
- "Good. This change improves task clarity because users can now distinguish the state through both label and icon."
- "Strong improvement. The chart is now readable through direct labels and pattern, not color alone."

## Milestone badge/title unlocked
**Inclusive Vision Designer**

# Unit-wide exercise bank

These exercise patterns should appear throughout the unit:
- identify the broken state
- compare normal and simulated views
- spot the task failure
- add one more cue
- redesign the chart
- repair the form
- choose the best backup signal
- order the review workflow

# Unit-wide design rules to reinforce
- do not rely on hue alone for meaning
- think in terms of user tasks, not just swatch appearance
- support color with labels, icons, patterns, borders, or position
- test common interface patterns under simulation
- remember that inclusive design helps more users than the obvious target group

# Suggested review prompts
Use these prompts in recap screens and review mode:
- What task becomes unclear when hue differences weaken?
- What second cue would make this state clearer?
- Does this chart still work without strong color distinction?
- Would this form still make sense if the border colors felt less distinct?
- Did you check the task, or only the palette?

# Suggested integration notes for the app
- The unit should use side-by-side comparison often.
- Visual changes should be immediate when switching simulation modes.
- Feedback should describe task breakdown clearly.
- Avoid medical overloading. The point is design action.
- The unit should prepare the learner for Unit 5 by building a strong habit of not relying on color alone.
